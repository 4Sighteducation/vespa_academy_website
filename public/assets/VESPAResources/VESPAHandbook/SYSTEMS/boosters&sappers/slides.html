Boosters & Sappers

SYSTEMS

Mandy Brown, leadership coach and former company boss

You can read Mandy's blog here: 'Energy Makes time'

Brown is interested in what gives us energy to achieve our goals.  

Many of us feel we have too much to do and not enough time to do it.  Brown argues that when our levels of energy are low, we often take much longer to do tasks that we might otherwise have completed quickly.  

Her advice is that we should stop thinking we don't have enough time, and instead start thinking about how to boost our energy so that we can make the best use of the time we have.  

How does Brown's theory relate to your school work?

If you were tired and lacking energy, you wouldn't be able to put in your personal best in a race (or even finish it!)

..or reading the relevant text book pages takes twice as long because you're not fully concentrating.  

Similarly, if you're feeling sluggish, the maths homework which should have taken you 30 mins ends up filling nearly an hour of your evening..

What's the solution?

You can maximise your energy by sequencing your tasks better.   

Brown explains that 'energy makes time'.  In other words, when you feel inspired or boosted, you'll be able to focus for longer, get more done and you'll seem to have more time (even if you're actually just using the time you've got more effectively).

So how do we organise our work in a way that boosts rather than saps our energy?

Write a list of five tasks which you might do in a day's work e.g. 

  • writing up a science practical
  • making notes from a textbook
  • practising for a music performance or language conversation session
  • completing maths problems
  • planning an essay
  • filing your notes
  • brainstorming ideas for a project

 

Now think about whether those tasks boost or sap your energy.  

Not sure?  Think about the following...

The booster

If you've completed an energy boosting task, you've got just as much (maybe even more!) energy after you finish. 

These tasks tend to leave you feeling positive, pleased and excited to do more.

Which tasks on your list make you feel this sort of energy and satisfaction? They won't necessarily be the easier subjects or tasks!  

The sapper

Working on energy sapping tasks feels the opposite. 

These tasks might not be difficult but they might leave you feeling flat and drained, as if your energy gauge is low, and you probably won't want to do any more work. 

Now look at your list of tasks...

For each task, try to assess how it is going to change your levels of energy.

Is it going to make you feel positive, energised and keen to do more?

Or is it going to leave you feeling flat, drained and lacking energy?

Draw the position of the needle on the revometer to show the energy levels of each task.  The red, right hand side is lots of energy; the blue, left hand side is lacking energy.  

What do you notice?  Can you spot any themes?

What sorts of tasks do you thrive doing? You look forward to them; feel satisfied after you've done them?

Which tasks require real grit to complete and can feel like an uphill climb?

What do you notice?

Now you've got this insight, you can organise and sequence a day's work that includes both boosters and sappers, but which means you don't run out of energy or motivation.  Here's how...

How to plan your day: the guidelines

Start your day with a booster.  Whatever happens afterwards, you've given yourself the energy to push forwards feeling positive.  

Try following a sapper with a booster -  If you've got a sapping task, it's good to have something to look forward to.  

Do two sappers with strict time limits, then give yourself a break.  Churning through a couple of tough tasks, and being strict about how much time you're going to spend doing them, deserves a reward.  Take a little time off, go for a walk, watch some TV, or play a game.  Then begin again with a boster.  

And finally...try finishing your day with a booster - You don't want that final task of the day extending out as you get more and more tired.  Aim to finish your day with something positive.  

Using the guidelines on the left, plan a study session to maximise and maintain your energy.  It could be a full day or a half day session.  

Now let's put it into practice....

1. Start your day with a booster.  

2. Do two sappers with strict time limits, then give yourself a break. 

3. Try following a sapper with a booster

4. Finish your day with a booster
