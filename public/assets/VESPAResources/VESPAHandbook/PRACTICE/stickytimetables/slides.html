CLASS POLL

Have you ever created / used  a revision timetable?

  1. I always create one, and I will use it and stick to it.

  2. I sometimes create one, and sometimes stick to it

  3. I have made them but can't ever stick to it

  4. I have  never made a revision timetable

Our own survey, of students across the UK found the following ....

How does this compare to your own class poll?

In our research, we found:

  • 97% of students have tried them

  • 3% of students have NEVER created one!

  • A huge 65% of students have made a revision timetable, but never stick to it

Making timetables that don't stick is a huge waste of time!

In the following video Educational Researcher Douglas Barton gives an interesting talk about revision. 

They design them incorrectly!

So what’s the mistake they make??

One of the highest impact activities his organisation found was ‘Designing and sticking to a revision timetable.’

They start by putting in the slots where they’re going to revise, and then try and fit real life around these revision sessions.

The trouble is, Barton says, huge proportions of students don’t stick to the schedule they’ve designed. 

As a result, these students create days and weeks that are dominated by revision...

He interviewed hundreds of students who abandon their timetables to find out why.

His conclusion?

...activities that might bring them joy, happiness, relaxation or connection, are squeezed out.

It becomes impossible to sustain and soon the timetable is consigned to the bin.

Designing Sticky Timetables

We think there are five things for you to focus on to create a successful timetable.

Once you have your template add all the compulsory time, ie. lessons, travel, etc

Here is an example to get you started...

Before you start you will need a template to plan your time...

1. Start by putting in key moments of connection, joy and relaxation

Consider -  sports activities, going to the gym, watching TV, meeting up with friends, a shift in a part-time job, etc

Aim for five or six moments of between one and two hours – totalling about ten-to-twelve hrs per week

Organise these so they’re not happening all at once. You might have 1hr on a Monday when you meet a friend....

...90mins on a Wednesday when you watch football, 1hr Thursday to go for a run, pizza night on Friday,Saturday into town!?

2.Organise work slots around  relaxation.

Check carefully that your relaxation slots aren’t crowding out your work.

 You’re aiming for balance, so that you’ve got plenty of time to do the work you need to do..

..but you’ve got these moments of celebration and freedom to keep you going.

3. Mentally rehearse the week.

For every tough period of revision, you need a reward (a mission then a medal)

...something small like a fifteen-minute break and a snack, a twenty-minute sit-com episode, a playlist of uplifting tunes!

Look as well at your longer periods of relaxation and joy. If your week doesn’t feel quite right in your head, look for tricky periods...

... is there too much relaxation happening all at once? Is there a run of three days that’s solid revision and needs breaking up?

You’re aiming for a week that feels challenging but do-able; a week that will ensure you do plenty of revision, but a moment of relaxation or joy is never far away

How could you adapt to 20 hours of independent study?

4. Run it through once, then adjust.

This is going to be a flexible and constantly changing document that responds to your progress and your levels of energy.

With each completed week, you’ve got a chance to redesign.

5. Ignore the setbacks.

There’s going to be days when you don’t follow your timetable...

If this happens, don’t beat yourself up. Just start again tomorrow as if nothing had gone wrong!

SUPERCHARGED REVISION PLAN

To supercharge your revision plan even further, try allocating 1hr to planning the following week in detail...

We suggest using 3 headings to plan each revision session. These will ensure you are well prepared and maximise the time during each revision slot.

Normal revision session plan

"Supercharged" session plan

SECTION 1 - HOW?

SECTION 2 - HOW LONG?

SECTION 3- HOW WILL I KNOW IT HAS BEEN EFFECTIVE?

In this section identify the topics you are going to cover and the specific activities you are going to do

Be very clear about the time. If you are doing active revision, spend no longer than 2 hrs. Always build in 10min breaks, every 50mins

Make sure you know you're making progress by testing yourself.

eg: A set of questions / A mini lesson you teach yourself / short answer questions under timed conditions

FINAL THOUGHTS

  • Have a go at designing one for yourself now.

  • You don’t need anything fancy and the internet is bursting with templates.

  • And anyway, as you know, it’s not the template that’s going to make a difference.

  • Just remember the four steps…

    1. Start by putting in some key moments of connection, joy and relaxation into your week.

    2. Now begin to organise the work-slots around these periods of relaxation.

    3. Mentally rehearse the week, thinking about ‘mission and ‘medal’.

    4. Run it through once, then adjust.​
