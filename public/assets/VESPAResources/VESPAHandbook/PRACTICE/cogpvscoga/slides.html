Cog P vs Cog A

PRACTICE

“The mind is not a vessel to be filled, but a fire to be kindled.”

Plutarch

Kathrin Stanger-Hall,

Biology Professor,

University of Georgia

...led a fascinating study, looking at the effect of different revision strategies, and their impact on exam results.

Those who knew all they had to do was to prepare for a multiple-choice exam became passive. They tended towards five revision strategies that were comfortable, repetitive and less challenging.

Have a guess at the types of activities they did?

Here are the activities they were observed doing....

  1. Reading the assigned text

  2. Re-reading class notes

  3. Making flash-cards of notes

  4. Highlighting key terms during reading

  5. Looking up difficult information

Those who knew they also faced short-answer questions, however, prepared very differently.... 

They were active; testing themselves more regularly and pushing themselves to do harder revision sessions.

Have a guess at the types of activities they did?

Here are the activities they were observed doing....

  1. Repeatedly asking/explaining “how does it work?” and “why does it work this way?”

  2. Creating and answering challenging study questions

  3. Closing notes and testing how much is remembered

  4. Drawing and labelling diagrams from memory

  5. Setting tests, trying to answer questions, then looking up information

Students were split into two groups. 

They were taught exactly the same material by the same teacher using the same resources – but here was the one difference... 

...the first group knew they were going to be tested at the end by a 90-question multiple-choice exam.

...the second group knew they’d take the same multiple-choice exam followed by a more challenging series of short-answer questions.

Off they went to revise for their exam, with the researchers watching them closely.....

 It turned out there was no difference in the amount of time they spent studying.

 However, there was a difference in how they approached their revision.

Cog P vs Cog A

Before we show you what happened to their results...

...think about the differences between these two approaches, and make some observations or suggestions about the impact they may have had...

What impact do you think each type of activity had on student results and motivation?

  • Cognitively Active group “…scored significantly higher on all questions.”

  • Cognitively Active group “…scored significantly higher on higher-level questions.”

  • Cognitively Active group “…learned significantly more, including critical-thinking skills.”

  • Cognitively Active group “…72.1% agreed or strongly agreed that they saw the value of learning.”

  • Cognitively Passive group…57.3% agreed or strongly agreed that they saw the value of learning.”

Findings

We think this image sums up how we should approach revision really nicely... 

.....What does it suggest to you?

  • Closing notes and testing how much is remembered

  • Creating and answering challenging study questions

  • Drawing and labelling diagrams from memory

  • Repeatedly asking/explaining “how does it work?” and “why does it work this way?”

  • Setting tests, trying to answer questions, then looking up information

Cognitively active revision builds deeper connections, and deeper understanding. That means using these techniques.

FINAL THOUGHTS

What does this teach us?

  • Cognitively active revision gets you better results, even if you spend the same amount of time doing it.

  • This means you might not have to do more revision to be successful…

  • you might just have to do different revision.

  • That’s great news for your work-life balance!
