What do your exams actually ask you to do?

We’ve worked with students who, despite revising hard, aren’t fully clear on what to expect on the day of the exam. 

In this activity we have gathered together nearly 100 command verbs from different GCSE and A level exam boards...

As a result they often have a meltdown or go blank.

​

But this doesn’t have to be you!!

...then considered a further 50 command verbs used in professional work-place exams.  

We're pretty sure most sections of your exams will be summarised in here somewhere, regardless of which subjects you take.

1. Define and describe things

Exams want us to be able to.......

1. Define and describe things

This could be history dates, people or places, parts of processes, complex vocabulary in full sentences a foreign language, subject-specific terms, one or two-step processes to solve a sum and so on.

2. Explain how things work, and show that we can use them to solve problems

Exams want us to be able to.......

2. Explain how things work, and show that we can use them to solve problems

You might have to arrive at an answer in a maths class by following a particular method, showing you know how stories work by writing your own, or using grammar correctly in translating a passage from French or German.

This could be saying how the water cycle works, how the heart beats or breathing works, or how something is built, like a poem or novel, or a business. 

3. Zoom into detail, analysing why things work the way they do

Exams want us to be able to.......

3. Zoom into detail, analysing why things work the way they do

Often you’ve got two things to handle at once; comparing two advertising campaigns in media or two businesses, or exploring why two characters from a book are similar or different.

..you could be translating and discussing a difficult passage, assessing a big table of data in science and comparing it to what you already know, or solving a complicated problem in maths using a number of steps. 

Here you might be pulling something apart to zoom in on it..for example - the images in a poem or the shape of a river valley in geography. 

4.Make judgements about things and justify opinions

Exams want us to be able to.......

4.Make judgements about things and justify opinions

You may be predicting ‘what will happen if…’ in psychology or economics or making recommendations about increasing profit in business studies or making judgements about the success or impact of novels or plays.

You might be adopting a point of view that isn’t yours in sociology, and arguing it through. 

In these questions you’re asked to offer an original opinion of your own.

You use what you know to justify your opinion; you might be using evidence to argue a particular point of view in history or geography.

Each one of these groups tends to come with its own "command verbs"

 ....words used in the exam that make it clear what type of question it is.

BIG IDEA..

1. Take a subject you’re studying and read a whole exam paper from beginning to end. Make a note of what every single question is asking you to do. Then see what types you’ve got.

Is it mostly one type? A scattering of types? Is there one area of questions you don’t have to worry about at all?

2. Have a look at the distribution of marks.

 

Do certain question types seem to have more marks attached to them?

 

This might tell you what the exam board thinks is important in your answers… and what skills they’re less concerned about.

3. Once you’ve done step one and two, you can set yourself better tests.

You don’t need to chase down every exam paper ever published, you can create your own exam that follows the rough shape of other papers.

Set and complete your own papers. Try our activity called - "Test Your Future Self" for help with this.

4. RAG - rate the question types you’re going to face – red for question-types you find really hard, amber for those in the middle, and green for the type of question you can breeze through.

 

Then adjust your revision so you’re spending more time on red question types.
