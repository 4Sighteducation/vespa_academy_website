Verbal Recaps

PRACTICE

Dr Becky Lawson, Psychologist, Liverpool University has helped inspire this activity..

Lawson has developed an interesting piece of research to measure how well we can explain things that we "think" we understand.

Lawson tests participants’ understanding by asking them to draw a bike!

What is striking about Lawson's results is that so many people have virtually no knowledge of how bicycles function.

Often we discover how much we know (or don't know) about something when we try to explain it to other people.

Can you spot the mistakes?

Here are some of the pictures produced by people who rated their knowledge of "how a bike works" as "Excellent" !!

Here are some Graduates,  given the same task!!

This activity uses Lawson's idea to help you identify your weaker areas when revising.

This tool is a habit-changer which will very quickly boost your understanding.

It takes very little effort but can yield big results.

 

Here's what to do... 

Every time you read a page of a textbook or study guide, stop and then...

VERBAL RECAPS

1. Close the book (or cover the notes you have been reading)

2. Calm your mind for a second and think – what did I just read?

3. Now talk aloud, summarising in your own words exactly what you’ve just been reading.
Try one of these starters to help:

 “The writer has just been explaining that…”
“This section explores…”
“The important idea here is that…”
“This page outlines the importance of…”
“The writer’s argument here is that…”
“I’ve been reading about how…”
“I’ve learnt that…”

4. If you can’t summarise it clearly…there’s been a problem. Not to worry. Go back and read the section again, and repeat step 3

 5. Once you're happy, try summarising the content, try asking and answering more complex questions; not ‘what’, but ‘why’ or ‘how’.

Try these:

  • “Why is this section important? Because…”

  • “Why has it been included? Well, it’s crucial because…”

  • “Why is this bit detailed… or why isn’t it detailed? I guess it’s because…”

  • “Why is the information in this order? The writer covers this first/second/third because…”

  • “How does it relate to the previous section? It’s linked in the following way…”

EXTENSION 1 - Spoken Chapter Summaries

1. Try adding spoken summaries of whole chapters, verbally once you’ve read them, like this...

  • “First, the writers explore….”

  • “…then they go on to argue that….”

  • “….then they look at -------- in more detail, explain how….”

  • “And they finish by concluding that….”

You’ll find that the information you read goes in. It sticks, and it’s easier to recall. Your reading might have taken a little longer, but you’ve been able to fully explain what it is you’ve just read.

Here are two extensions to try… 

Reading like this means you go slower. But it means you’re testing yourself as you go along. 

EXTENSION 2 - Teach your imaginary Class

  • This afternoon I’m going to be talking you through ______________.

  • This part of the course is crucial because…

  • The key definitions you’ll need to be able to handle are….

  • The big ideas that you’ll need to be confident about are…

  • The exam is going to ask you to __________, so pay particular attention to ____________

  • OK. So Let’s get started by looking at _________

  • Try putting together a short lecture as if to an imaginary class.

  • If you are stuck, we suggest using our script

  • You’ll quickly realise that you really need to know your topic in order to deliver a  mini-lecture like this!

  • It’s a good one to do in a study group – each member takes responsibility for one topic, and presents it using the following script

2. Try teaching the content to other students, or simply to your bedroom wall!!

FINAL THOUGHTS

  • This activity may feel uncomfortable or embarrassing at first

  • However it is one of the simplest methods to learn and memorise important information.

  • The act of speaking information "into the world" makes it come to life.

  • It is no longer just thoughts and concepts in your head!!

  • The slight feeling of pressure when you do this stimulates your brain to focus and remember.

  • You could even just speak out loud to your bedroom wall, and no one ever needs to know!!
