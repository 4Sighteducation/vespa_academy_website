A QUESTION OF MONEY

A QUESTION OF MONEY

Recent studies in motivation in the work place suggest that money doesn't necessarily improve performance, or make people happier at work!

Seperate experiments conducted in the US and India came to the same conclusion.....

...When the task is dull and repetitive, money provides a resonable motivation to improve

...But when the task is complex - like most tasks in the modern workplace - more money doesn't mean better outcomes.

Daniel Pink

Author, Political Speechwriter, Journalist

...Wrote an excellent book on motivation called Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us.

 

 

In it he looks in detail at the idea of money and how it is used to incentivise us in the workplace.  The following videos outline his ideas...

Riccio et al. (2013) 11,000 NY students are offered $300 for ‘passing’ or $600 for ‘passing with proficiency’ on standardised exams

Angrist, Oreopoulos, Williams (2014) Canadian university students are offered $100 for grades higher than 70%, with $20 per 1% they are higher

Leuven, Oosterbeck, Van Der Klaauw (2010) Dutch students at the University of Amsterdam are offered $600 to complete ‘all their first-year requirements’

Springer, Rosenquist and Swain (2014) Students are offered optional support classes and are split into 2 groups. Group 1 got a certificate for attending 25% of classes. Group 2 got $25 if they attended 75%

No

significant difference

Some positive difference

Bettinger (2012): Primary school pupils in Ohio are offered $20 for each ‘advanced score’ in reading, writing, maths and science

EDUCATIONAL STUDIES LOOKING AT THE EFFECT OF MONEY AS A SOURCE OF MOTIVATION

One of the ONLY educational studies to have found a positive difference in student motivation, when money is offered to students is this one.

Even in this study the greater impact was found to be on the students receiving a certificate that they could share with their parents because this was related to their INTRINSIC motivation

So what matters to you...

(A thought experiment!)

  • Imagine you’ve got a job that pays you very well.

 

  • You can buy everything you need and you have no money worries.

 

  • So given that, what would be important to you in this job?

Of the following thirty possibilities try one of the following:

1 - Choose your top 3 or top 5

2 - Put a top 10 in order of preference or

3 - Put all 30 in order of importance to you.

 

.. feel free to add any other things we missed and add them yourself...

  • To face unfamiliar situations and improvise solutions in the moment,

  • To organise your own day on your own terms,

  • To be making a difference in people’s health, wellbeing or happiness,

  • To be creative, producing new and original content,

  • To have status, power and expertise,

  • To solve problems for others, improving their lives,

  • To have a safe role, a job for life with no threat of ever being made redundant,

  • To wake early to an alarm clock and be up and active before everyone else,

  • To wake up every day excited for the day’s work ahead,

  • To be finished at 5:00pm and not think about work until the next morning,

  • To be travelling from place to place,

  • To work in the same place each day, with the same commute and same workspace,

  • To develop new skills through practice and feedback,

  • To share your enthusiasm for something you’re passionate about,

  • To have time to laugh and joke; to not take work seriously,

  • To lead others (making decisions about who does what),

  • To have someone else make all the decisions and tell you what to do,

  • To use your hands to practically build, maintain or fix something,

  • To be operating in a high-stakes, high-pressure environment

  • To lead a low-stakes, stress-free life doing something unimportant,

  • To be in a busy, bustling atmosphere with music, activity, conversation,

  • To have predictable working hours with a set start and finish time,

  • To have a list of predictable tasks – every day roughly the same,

  • To stay late into the night, still focused long after others have gone home,

  • To be active, moving, physical… to be away from a desk,

  • To have a space of your own, and projects you complete entirely by yourself,

  • To be helping to change people’s opinions, attitudes and behaviours,

  • To dress however you want,

  • To respond to crises and emergencies as and when they come up,

  • To have every day different.
