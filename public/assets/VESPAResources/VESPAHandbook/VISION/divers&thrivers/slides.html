Diver goals and Thriver goals

Imagine you have a group of university students.  They are all on the same course at the same institution, and they all got the required grades for that course.

Is it possible to predict who will get the best results at the end of the course?

 If so, what do you think would give you clues?

Researchers from the Universities of Pittsburgh and Toronto wanted to understand what made students in their first year of university particularly successful (or not).  

  1. What are your two most inspiring goals?

  2. What kind of person do you want to be later in life?

  3. What qualities do you admire in others?

Predicting Success

Their research focused on 3000 economics undergraduates who all had really good college (A Level) grades.  The questionnaire asked them about their hopes and dreams:

A year later, the researchers took the students who had ended up in the top 10% of their year group, and compared them to the students in the bottom 10%.  

Both groups of students had set themselves goals.  But there was a marked difference between the type of goals each group of students had set.

Examples of Goals Set..

Bottom 10% of Students

These are called -

"DIVER GOALS"

  • They focused on "Being" or "Becoming" something... usually rich, or

  • "having" or "receiving" something - money, power, particular job, or status.

  • These goals were about an outcome, not a journey.

  • ‘Be rich’

  • ‘Get rich quickly’

  • ‘Being successful’

  • ‘Having so many successful businesses’

  • ‘Have my own company have my own house and car’

  • ‘Receive a high level of education’

  • ‘Be an actuary’

  • ‘build something’

  • ‘contribute to the human advancement of…’

  • ‘I want to try something different’

  • ‘…fix people’s problems’

  • ‘independent person who can deal with problems’

  • ‘working in the field of Computer Science’

  • ‘build a strong foundation to succeed’

Examples of Goals Set..

Top 10% of Students

These are called -

"THRIVER GOALS"

  • These placed an emphasis on activity and sense of purpose - "building", "contributing", "trying", "fixing" or generally dealing with problems 

  • These were goals that didn't specify an outcome, but gave detail about a process or a journey

The outcomes of the research were clear...

The students with thriver goals were significantly more successful.  They avoided procrastination and worked harder to achieve their goals.

So, what goals do you have?

There’s nothing wrong with wanting wealth and riches. The problem comes where your goal is just about wealth and riches, and doesn’t emphasise action. As if the wealth and riches are a magical outcome that just happens.

This study showed that students in the bottom 10% actually spent longer thinking about their futures than top 10% students did – but they were just dreaming about what it might be like to be a millionaires. They did less work than their peers.

Try these starters to help:

  • I want to build…

  • I want to help solve the problem of..

  • I want to tackle the issue of…

  • I want to work every day in the ----  industry

  • I want to deal with the issue of…

  • I want to help people…

Reframing your life goals....

So instead, frame your goal in terms of what you might want to do. Emphasise action – the journey, the process.

FINAL THOUGHTS

  • Remember, if you set goals like this, you’ve still every chance you’ll end up very wealthy and successful.

  • You’ll also have the advantage of knowing a little about how you’re going to make a change in the world.

  • It’s a win-win situation.
