Proactive vs Reactive

EFFORT

Comprehensive 

There are 2 types of work you do on any course....

1. REACTIVE - Completed in response to instruction, eg.classwork or whomework etc

2. PROACTIVE - Work you set yourself, because you know it will help

PROACTIVE vs REACTIVE

Entirely Reactive

Mostly reactive
Proactive on rare occasions

A balance of reactivity and proactivity

Some reactivity, but mostly proactive

These students only complete work if they’re told to.

They’ve often never set themselves any work – even in the run-up to exams, they go to extra classes and react to the instructions they get there.

These students complete almost all their work because they’re told to.
Now and again, if there’s a crisis, they’ll spend a small amount of time proactively – revising for a test or tracking down some missing notes.

These students are close to matching their reactive work with proactive work.
They’re regularly setting themselves work; re-reading and tidying notes, asking questions, reading textbooks and submitting redone essays.

These students get their reactive study out of the way pretty quickly, completing it to a high standard so they can get on with more proactive work.
They enjoy the proactive work, exploring topics in detail and challenging themselves.

1. Tidying and re-organising your notes 8. Seeking a book/study guide recommendation from a teacher 15. Beginning a personal project to explore a topic studied at a level above yours
2. Borrowing someone’s notes 9. Handing in a re-done piece of work 16. Contacting employers or employees to ask questions
3. Reviewing your feedback to look for patterns 10. Sending five emails asking for support, help, advice or an opportunity 17. Volunteering to teach someone else the topics you know inside out
4. Handing a piece of homework in early and asking for advice on how to improve it before deadline day 11. Asking five complex questions of a teacher and noting down the answers 18. Seeking out a reading list for a subject at a higher level than yours
5. Completing a one-hour re-read and re-organise of notes on any topic 12. Listening to a podcast related to a topic you’ve studied 19. Entering a competition or challenge
6. Attending a support class or revision session 13. Watching a video-summary of a topic, making fresh notes as you go. 20. Organising a study-visit to an employer or place of study
7. Summarising a topic in a single page of notes and diagrams 14. Seeking out three short exam questions related to a topic and completing them under timed conditions 21. Interviewing a student working at a higher level than you and summarising their advice in notes.

GETTING STARTED AND BUILDING UP...

In the early stages of learning to be proactive, try and complete one or two proactive tasks a week, spending about half an hour on each. Choose the ‘easy’ ones.

1) Starting Out..

Once you’re more confident, you can up your proactive hours, closing the gap between your reactive study and proactive study until they’re balanced. 

2) Aiming for a balance..

You might be able to do this by completing loads of the ‘easy’ tasks above, but the likelihood is you’ll be doing some ‘medium’ ones too.

You’ll begin to see your grades improve – your teachers might even express some surprise at how well you’re doing!

Once you’re close to a balance of reactive and proactive, try introducing a few of the tasks labelled ‘challenges’ into your working week. 

3) Hitting your stride..

By this time, you’ll be regularly performing well in tests and exams and should feel much more optimistic and confident!

"The proactive person doesn't wait for success to come to them; they go out and make it happen."

John C Maxwell -  American author, speaker, and pastor
