Becoming Indistractible

The idea  for this activity comes from the work of Nir Eyal and his book - Indistractible" 

Eyal  explores why it is we find our lives controlled by tech giants thousands of miles away even when we have something important to do right in front of us.

Often our time slips away in a haze of checking social media, sending text messages and looking at cat videos…

before we know it, we’ve spent two hours half-concentrating on something we could have finished in thirty minutes if we’d only been fully focussed.

We’ve found that for many students, the key to increasing levels of effort is decreasing levels of distraction.

The key to increasing levels of effort is decreasing levels of distraction!

Becoming Indistractible

Eyal argues we should recognise that discomfort (ie.something feeling hard or boring) precedes distraction...

..so we need to be ready for it!

Whenever we sit down to do work, we need to admit that we will:  

  1. Feel uncomfortable

  2. Look for distractions.

Once we’ve admitted this to ourselves, we can...

"Anticipate distraction."

So where are the distractions coming from??

Indistractible  Spaces

  • Where are your Quiet Calm Spaces?

  • .....which promote concentration

  • .....feature none of the devices or people we associate with distraction

  • Ask yourself:

    • What might this look like?

    • What might it contain?

    • Where might it be?

    • When might it be at its best for you?

One of the solutions to our external triggers is to design "indistractable spaces".

These are spaces that are quiet and calm – spaces that promote concentration and feature none of the devices or people we associate with distraction.

Design your Indistractable Space Now...

Indistractible  Routines

  • Consider:

    • How long do you need, and how would the breaks work? - e.g.

      • Twenty minutes of work with a five minute break?

      • Thirty minutes on, then fifteen off?

    • Think about chunking the session so that you move through phases. Could you do:

    • Fifteen minutes note-taking then a fifteen minute exam question? .... or twenty minutes preparation for a ten-minute test?

    • Finally, consider the rewards that might come with successful completion. A ten-minute check of social? A walk and a chat? A cup of coffee?

Eyal argues most internal triggers are sudden urges to do something else....

....However these urges typically pass within ten minutes. Indistractable routines help with this.

By designing your study periods in advance, to include breaks, you will maximise your chances of concentrating for the full session

Design your Indistractable Routine Now...

FINAL THOUGHTS

Combining the Two..​

  • Once you’ve got your indistractable space and your indistractable routine, put the two together.

  • Schedule a study session for some time in the next day or two, and try it out.

  • You never know – it might mean you get way more done in less time!
