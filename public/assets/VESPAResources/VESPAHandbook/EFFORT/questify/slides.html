1. Location - the best quests take you away from familiar spaces

In 1979, three American students decided they needed a place off campus to study.  They dragged this desk up a hill to a height of 155m so that they could work without distractions in the silence of the Texan desert!

Location can help you find

your 'flow'

Recent research has looked at 'flow' i.e. a high concentration state which allows us to achieve high quality work.  You might call it being 'in the zone'.

You may have experienced flow if you've been so absorbed in a task that time seems to fly.

 

Flow is achieved when the task is challenging AND when we are entirely focused on it. 

Find the best 'flow' location

Low flow spaces tend to have: High flow spaces tend to have:

  • lots of background noise

  • people moving around

  • distractions

  • cramped

  • too hot or cold

  • low lighting

  • are quiet or have non-intrusive background noise

  • there's nobody you know to chat to

  • light and airy

  • enough working space

Make a list of possible 'high flow' spaces you could use.  

Think about spaces in school and local libraries.  

If you're considering a coffee shop, think about where you will sit.  In a corner at the back might be better than in the window or by the counter.

Theory of delayed gratification

But don't let eating your snack be another procrastination technique!  Give yourself milestones as to when you can eat that chocolate bar.  

The famous Mischel experiment showed that students who could delay gratification i.e. wait for a treat tended to be more successful in school.  They were able to prioritise the bigger reward at the end and work towards a longer term goal.

So think:  I can have that chocolate bar... but only when I've written the first three paragraphs of my essay.

3. Publicity

Tell your friends, family (and anyone who'll listen) that you're going off to do battle with this terrible task.  

Warn them they won't see you until evening, when it's done.  

Now you can't bail out without looking like a wuss! 

To successfully do battle on your quest, you must break contact with the outside world.  Put that phone on silent!

4. Radio Silence

What's allowed

 

  • study playlists - search 'music for concentration'

  • a call to arrange a lift home WHEN YOU'RE FINISHED

 

 

What's NOT allowed

  • Your usual choice of music (sorry, but it's scientifically proven that anything with lyrics takes up brain capacity and is actually distracting)

  • social media

  • phone alerts

4. Radio Silence

4. Radio Silence

Research from the University of Texas found that students who left their phone in their bag rather than on the desk had more cognitive capacity (i.e. did better in tests) even if all participants had their phone on silent.

And the students who did the best in tests were those who had their phone in another room rather than with them.  

 

Trying to ignore your phone takes up brain capacity which you could be using for your work.  So if you must have your phone with you, make sure it's out of sight and out of mind.
