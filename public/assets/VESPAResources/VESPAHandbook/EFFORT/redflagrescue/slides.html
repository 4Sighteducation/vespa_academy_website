Red Flag and Rescue Plans

EFFORT

New good habits

If you've begun a new work habit, well done!

Maybe you're putting in more effort in class, taking part in class discussions, completing homework as thoroughly as you can, asking for additional feedback, doing some extra reading.

It was going really well... for a while.  But now you can feel yourself slipping back to your old ways. 

It's normal for motivation to fade... but it doesn't have to be fatal to your new good habit.

Motivation slipping?

If you feel your motivation ebbing away, that you're slipping back into old habits, you can rescue yourself.  

Create your red flag rescue plan now (just in case!)

By looking out for the warning signs and acting quickly, you can get yourself back on track and in a winning position.

Understand when you're in danger

When a good habit fades, it doesn't suddenly collapse in one disastrous afternoon.  It falls apart in two stages...

Stage 1: small slips and excuses

Slips feel insignificant to start with.  They happen now and again and we often find ourselves excusing them.  'It's no big deal.'

BUT, that's only true as long as you spot them and adjust.  

What sort of slips should you look out for?

Stage 1: Be alert to your small slips

Small slips and excuses

  • you let one lesson go by and realise you weren't paying attention

  • you haven't engaged in classroom discussions or activities

  • there's something you don't understand but you've done nothing about it

  • you rushed your homework and know it's nowhere near your best

  • your gaming and TV time has gone up 

  • you're avoiding doing homework

If your motivation is slipping, what does that look like?

Stage 2: Watch out for red flags

A number of small slips accumulate over a few days or weeks.  Things start to feel more chaotic.

Look out for the danger signs:

  • you notice three lessons have gone by and you've switched off in all of them, missing some crucial stuff

  • a topic seems to be hard but for a few days you've just ignored it

  • you've skipped study sessions and haven't gone back to check over class notes for almost a week

  • you're using social media, games, videos and other distractions more regularly

This is where things are getting more serious.

As soon as you notice a red flag, you need to start your rescue procedure.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The rescue plan

What could you do to help yourself get back on track?

  • stay behind after school (or go in early) for a couple of days, go to the library and do some rescue work

  • speak to a teacher, admit you're struggling and ask what tasks to do to fill in gaps in your understanding

  •  go through a friend's notes

  • ask a classmate to talk you through a difficult section

  • attend a lunchtime catch up or support session

  • use online resources recommended by your teacher to revisit the topic

The reassurance of a rescue plan

Students with clear red flags and well-organised rescue plans have two advantages over other students:

  1. if you've got a list of red flag indicators to watch out for, you're more likely to notice when a good habit is at risk of failing

  2. you have a list of actions to complete as a ready-made solution to the red flag, so you're quicker at solving the problem.
